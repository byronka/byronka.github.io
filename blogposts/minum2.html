What sets Minum apart

February 11, 2024

To those programmers who yearn for minimalism, and think highly of Java, I would like to clue you in to a framework I am maintaining. It is called Minum, and it has everything necessary to build a website. It is based on a preference to spend extra time at the onset, to avoid common maintenance difficulties later.

What sets Minum apart

A website has typical components:

  1. A web server: for transmitting data using correct protocol.
  2. Routing: for conveying data from the web to the business code and back.
  3. A database: for persisting data in an organized way to disk.
  4. Logging: to consistently generate runtime output.
  5. Templating: Inserting data into outgoing documents, like HTML.
  6. Testing: to test out parts of the program.
  7. Background job processing.
  8. HTML parsing

Minum contains all these, without any third-party dependencies.

There are many Java web frameworks using adjectives like lightweight, micro, minimalist, and simple. However, it is hard to find one that takes those ideas to their logical conclusion. Minum was written from scratch, with minimalism as its north star. There are no third-party dependencies other than the JDK itself!

For those not yet on the minimalism train, you might be wondering what the value is.

When you encounter difficulty with dependencies, what is your likeliest course of action? Maybe reviewing the documentation (if it exists) or online forums. Barring that, it may be necessary to read the code. It is often difficult to achieve a confident result when reading the code of a project that (including dependencies) is hundreds of thousands of lines. Let me write that a different way, just to make sure it is clear. Most of the so-called "minimalist" web frameworks are between 400,000 and 700,000 lines of code when you account for the lines of code of the dependencies necessary for a fully-fledged outcome, and that is being conservative - databases are routinely more than a million lines of code.

Imagine instead that all the code for those components was a total of 4000 lines of code. Then, if something misbehaves, there is a much higher likelihood you can find the issue.

Another point: Many professional projects enter a period of high churn and poor quality once they get past their early days. Just my opinion: a lot of the mindshare in our industry right now is on techniques and technologies that give high returns at the onset, for a high maintenance cost later. Of course I cannot speak for everyone's situation, but I have seen this enough times in my career to hop on the minimalism bandwagon.

Great effort was put towards testing and documentation. It is possible to verify the system for yourself upon download from Github, merely by running "mvn test" - it should take less than 15 seconds.

An example of a larger-than-toy application is available for review at GitHub
